Does fashion actually trickle down?

An ongoing field study · July 2026 · updated nightly by the pipeline
runway shows → high-street racks → fiber mills, measured

1.The question

the 1899 “trickle-down” theory, finally with receipts nobody keeps old Zara pages… except the Internet Archive

In March, Prada sends wool coats down a runway in Milan. The theory — it goes back to Veblen — says that look works its way down: into Zara and H&M within a few seasons, into what people search for, and eventually into order books at the mills that spin the fiber. Everyone in fashion repeats some version of this. As far as we could find, nobody had actually measured it end to end.

So we did. And since the fashion press can't tell you whether any of it is tradeable, we also wired the whole thing to stock prices and made the math show its work.

2.How we measured it

each archive snapshot is timestamped — we only ever “know” a fact after its date. no hindsight allowed
step 1 · the runway
26,855 looks
Every outfit from 22 major houses across 11 years of fashion weeks, scraped from Vogue Runway. A vision model looks at each photo and estimates the fabric mix — "wool 0.6, silk 0.4". 8,670 tagged so far; the rest are being worked through nightly.1
step 2 · the high street
3,762 fabric labels
What did Zara stock in March 2018? We dug 5,790 product pages out of the Internet Archive (H&M 2,216, ASOS 1,448, UNIQLO 98) and read the composition field off each one — "87% cotton, 13% elastane" — month by month back to 2016-01.2
step 3 · the money
27 tickers
Google search interest per fabric, listed fast-fashion retailers, the fiber makers (polyester, viscose, nylon), and the commodities behind them — cotton futures, crude, live cattle.

Then one guardrail over everything: a fact only counts from the day we could have known it — the show date, the snapshot date, the filing date. That single rule is what separates a measurement from a story told in hindsight.

3.What we found

read it top to bottom: runway → shops → mills. wider thread = more of the season the faded stub is honesty: elastane has no reliable lag yet

Fashion does trickle down, and you can clock it. Each thread below is one material in the current season (FW2026); it drops from the runway band to the high-street band at its measured lag — where the thread lands and the label at the bend tell you how many months and how tight the fit is.3

The headline numbers: wool shows up in search interest about 5 months after it walks (r = 0.63 across 78 months); nylon takes ~9; cotton ~4. ASOS's rack composition follows the runway's denim share about 4 months later (r = 0.52 over 25 measured months). And at the far end of the chain, Indorama Ventures, the world's biggest polyester maker tracks the high-street polyester tilt about 6 months later (r = 0.59).

Which makes the current season interesting: wool is 28% of FW2026 — +9.3 points above its three-season average — the biggest jump on the board. If the lags keep holding, that shift reaches consumer demand around September 2026.

4.The part where we don't make money (yet)

a system that can say “no” is the only kind whose “yes” means anything

We built the obvious trading strategy on top of this — tilt toward the fiber makers when their material is heating up downstream — and tested it the way you'd test a drug: rules registered before each run, walk-forward validation on 2017–2022, and 2023–2025 locked in a sealed test window that the code physically refuses to open until a model earns it.

So far, none has. All nine parameter combinations lost money in validation (best information ratio -0.60); the naive version returned -45% over seven years with a 33% hit rate. We publish that number at the same size as the good ones.4

Why be this strict? Because with enough retries, random noise will hand you a beautiful backtest. The propagation lags in section 3 survive this regime; a tradeable edge hasn't yet. Both statements are worth exactly as much because they were produced the same way.

5.Still on the loom

this page rebuilds itself every night at 6:30

The archive dig is roughly half done (87 retailer-months reconstructed) and the vision tagging is at 32%. As those fill in, the retailer-level questions unlock: who copies the runway fastest, and is their speed priced? If a strategy ever clears validation, it gets one — exactly one — shot at the sealed 2023–2025 window, and the result goes here, whichever way it lands.
