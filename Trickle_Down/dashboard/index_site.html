Overview

A field study · runway → high street → fiber mills

A 127-year-old idea about fashion, finally measured.

In March, Prada sends wool down a runway in Milan. The claim that the look works its way down — into Zara and H&M, into what people search for, eventually into the mills that spin the fiber — is old and never checked. We checked it: 26,855 runway looks, a decade of fast-fashion assortment rebuilt from the Internet Archive, and the lag between each tier, measured.

The cascade, drawn from the fitted numbers — not an illustration. Each thread is one material; its reach is the measured lag from the runway to consumer search or retail racks.

How we measured it

01 · The runway
26,855 looks
22 houses, 23 seasons of shows (2015–2026), each machine-tagged for the fabrics it puts on the catwalk. 26,838 tagged so far.
02 · The high street
11,229 fabric labels
read off archived Zara, H&M, ASOS and Uniqlo product pages recovered from the Internet Archive — 121 retailer-months back to 2016-01. Every snapshot is stamped with the day it was live, so nothing here uses hindsight.
03 · The money
4 fiber makers
Google search interest for every material, plus prices for the listed suppliers that spin the fiber. We line the three tiers up and measure the lag between them.

The Honest Part

How this is measured

Runway shows lead high-street material mixes, which lead the demand priced into fiber suppliers. This cascade is measured, not assumed: runway looks are vision-tagged per material; retailer mixes are reconstructed from Wayback-archived product pages, each row timestamped by its snapshot — no look-ahead; propagation is fitted on a 2017–2022 training window with walk-forward folds (validating 2020, 2021, 2022); 2023–2025 is a sealed test window, evaluated once with frozen parameters. Every methodology decision is pre-registered in DECISIONS.md before the run that uses it. Negative results are published with equal prominence.

Cross-validation — every combination, every fold
